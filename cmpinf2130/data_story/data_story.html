Fentanyl Overdose Deaths in Allegheny County, Pennsylvania

Trends, Geographic Spread, and Unequal Impact (2007–2025)

by Robert

Created: April 19, 2026

Introduction

Between 2014 and 2025, Allegheny County, Pennsylvania, experienced a dramatic surge in fatal drug overdoses, driven almost entirely by fentanyl. What began as a crisis involving heroin and prescription opioids quickly evolved into a far deadlier epidemic once fentanyl entered the local drug supply.

This data story is designed for Allegheny County residents, public health officials, and policymakers. Its purpose is to reveal how fentanyl has transformed the overdose crisis and to advocate for expanded, equitable harm reduction efforts that can prevent future deaths.

This story examines the epidemic through three perspectives:

  • When and what happened: the explosive rise of fentanyl.
  • Where the deaths spread geographically.
  • Who has been impacted, including racial disparities.

The following three visualizations explore these perspectives in detail.

Data Sources & Methods

Data for this story come from the Allegheny County Fatal Accidental Overdoses Dataset, maintained by the Western Pennsylvania Regional Data Center (WPRDC). Overdose records spanning 2007-2025 were cleaned and analyzed in Python using pandas and geopandas. Visualizations were created with matplotlib and seaborn. Population-adjusted rates were calculated using U.S. Census Bureau American Community Survey (ACS) 2019–2023 5-Year Estimates for Allegheny County demographics.

1. When & What: The Explosive Rise of Fentanyl

From 2007 to 2013, fentanyl (including analogs) was a relatively minor contributor to overdose deaths in Allegheny County. Starting in 2014, the situation changed dramatically: fentanyl quickly entered the top five drugs and within just two years, overtook every other substance to become the leading cause of fatal overdoses.

As shown in the time-series chart below, fentanyl-related deaths surged sharply after 2014 while deaths involving other drugs declined significantly after 2016. The chart highlights how fentanyl peaked in 2021, accounting for more deaths than all other drugs combined. Although numbers have since fallen toward 2016 levels, this visualization demonstrates that fentanyl remains the single leading cause of drug overdose deaths in the county as of 2024.

Fentanyl (including analogs) rapidly became the leading cause of fatal overdoses after 2014, peaking in 2021.

2. Where: The Geographic Spread

From 2007 to 2013, fentanyl-related deaths were rare and sporadically scattered across only a few isolated areas of Allegheny County.

The animated map below reveals how this pattern changed once fentanyl entered the local drug supply in 2014. As the animation plays, watch the rapid geographic expansion unfold in waves, with intense hotspots quickly emerging and intensifying particularly on Pittsburgh’s South Side, North Side, and South Hills. This visualization clearly illustrates the transition from isolated cases to widespread hotspots across the county, highlighting how quickly the drug had spread throughout the county.

Fentanyl Overdose Deaths Spread Quickly from Isolated Areas into Major Hotspots Across Allegheny County (2007–2025)

Fentanyl, once a relatively nonexistent problem, rapidly spread and intensified across Allegheny County, hitting certain neighborhoods particularly hard.

3. Who: The Unequal Impact

White residents account for the largest number of fentanyl-related deaths across all age groups, largely because they make up a bigger share of the county’s population.

However, as the paired bar charts below make clear, raw counts can be misleading. The left panel displays absolute death counts, while the right panel shows rates adjusted for population size. This adjustment reveals that Black residents experience significantly higher death rates than White residents in nearly every age group. The disparity is especially pronounced among working-age adults (45–65+), where Black residents' rates are often two to three times higher.

Racial Disparities in Fentanyl Overdose Deaths: High Absolute Counts for White Residents, Much Higher Rates for Black Residents

Left: White residents have the highest absolute number of deaths. Right: Black residents experience significantly higher death rates than White residents in nearly every age group. (Population demographics are based on 2019–2023 ACS 5-Year Estimates for the 2014–2025 death data. "Other Races" shown only in raw counts due to accounting for less than 2% of the reported data).

4. Conclusion & Recommendations

As the time-series chart, animated map, and side-by-side bar charts collectively demonstrate, fentanyl has dramatically transformed the overdose crisis in Allegheny County.

While White residents account for the largest absolute number of deaths due to their larger population share, Black residents face substantially higher death rates (often 2 to 3 times higher) in most age groups.

This crisis is not evenly distributed. It has clear geographic patterns and deep racial disparities. These deaths are largely preventable through expanded access to naloxone, fentanyl test strips, treatment, and equitable harm reduction strategies targeted at the hardest-hit neighborhoods and communities.

Policymakers and public health officials should prioritize these evidence-based interventions where the need and potential impact is greatest.
