1. When & What: The Explosive Rise of Fentanyl

From 2007 to 2013, fentanyl (including analogs) was a relatively minor contributor to overdose deaths in Allegheny County. The situation changed dramatically in 2014, when fentanyl entered the top five drugs. Within just two years, it had overtaken every other substance to become the leading cause of fatal overdoses.

Between 2016 to 2020, deaths involving other drugs declined significantly, while fentanyl-related deaths continued to rise. Fentanyl reached its peak in 2021, accounting for more deaths than all other drugs combined. After the peak, fentanyl deaths fell back toward 2016 levels. In 2024, fentanyl caused roughly the same number of deaths as cocaine, yet it remains the single leading cause of drug overdose deaths in the county.

Fentanyl (including analogs) has rapidly became the leading cause of fatal overdose in Allegheny County.

2. Where: The Geographic Spread

From 2007 to 2013, fentanyl-related deaths were rare and sporadically scattered across only a few isolated areas of Allegheny County.

Starting in 2014, the first clear signs emerged that fentanyl had entered the local drug supply network. What followed was a rapid geographic expansion. Fentanyl deaths did not stay contained — they spread and intensified across many Allegheny County zip codes over time, hitting certain neighborhoods particularly hard.

Main Takeaway

Fentanyl has dramatically transformed the overdose crisis in Allegheny County.

While White residents account for the largest absolute number of deaths due to their larger population share, Black residents face substantially higher death rates (often 2 to 3 times higher) in most age groups.

This crisis is not evenly distributed. It has clear geographic patterns and deep racial disparities. These deaths are largely preventable through expanded access to naloxone, fentanyl test strips, treatment, and equitable harm reduction strategies.
